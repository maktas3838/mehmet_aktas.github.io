Research Summary
  My research is in the general area of applied and computational topology. My work focuses on using computational tools in the study topological data analysis and applied network science.
  My research integrates concepts from statistics and computational topology to analyze network data sets, more specifically hypergraphs. Hypergraphs are successfully used to model higher-order interactions in complex systems where entities are represented as nodes, and higher-order interactions among them are represented as hyperedges. They are able to represent complex systems more faithfully than graphs since graphs are limited to pairwise relationships between entities and we cannot describe all relations as pairwise relations in complex systems, rather should describe them as higher-order interactions. While hypergraphs are data structures in the world of network models, they are also closely related to higher-order mathematical objects from topology, the branch of mathematics that studies the properties of the shapes that are invariant under continuous deformation. The topological features arising from these objects can benefit many different hypergraph mining problems. In my research, I have been striving to develop novel topology-based hypergraph methodologies that facilitate efficient analysis of large hypergraphs and advance a wide spectrum of applications. Employing the topological methods on hypergraphs will enable to extract and use different topological structures in various benchmark hypergraph mining tasks, such as hypergraph classification and hypergraph embeddings.

Selected Research Projects

Influeantial node and higher-order interactions detection on networks. Identifying influential nodes in a complex network is an important graph mining problem with many diverse applications such as information propagation, market advertising, and rumor controlling. Influential nodes in a network play critical roles and largely affect network structure and functions than the rest of the network. They are able to diffuse or spread information throughout a complex network more rapidly. In this project, we detect the important nodes and higher-order interactions in networks that have key roles in information diffusion.


Modeling diffusion on Hypergraphs Diffusion on networks is an important concept in network science that models how a stuff, such as information and heat, diffuses between vertices based on network topology, the pattern of who is connected to whom. In this project, we develop new hypergraph Laplacians to model diffusion more comprehensively.


Hypergraph classification. Modern science and technology have witnessed in the past decade a proliferation of complex data that can be naturally modeled and interpreted as networks. For example, one can represent proteins as hypergraphs where nodes represent secondary structure elements and hyperedges indicate neighborhood in the amino-acid sequenxe or in 3D space. In this project, we use TDA to classify biological and chemical networks such as whether or not chemical compounds have a mutagenic effect on a specific bacteria, or for activity agains non-small cell lung cnacer and overian cancer cell lines.
